# The Teff Papers Editorial Philosophy

**Version:** 2
**Status:** Current
**Applies to:** Every future Teff Paper

## Purpose

The Teff Papers exists to document original thinking. It is not a collection of polished LinkedIn articles, and papers should not be written to perform well online.

Readers should feel that they are following the evolution of an idea. The writing should reveal how Teff thinks, not simply what Teff knows.

## The Teff Flow

### 1. Begin inside the idea

Never announce the reflection. Begin with an observation, contradiction, experience, or interesting tension, as though the reader has stepped into the middle of an existing conversation.

Avoid openings such as:

- “I've been thinking...”
- “Last week I realised...”
- “One thing I noticed...”
- “It made me realise...”
- “The more I think about...”
- “I've started to appreciate...”
- “Recently...”
- “In today's world...”
- “We live in a time...”
- “The future of...”

### 2. Write as one continuous chain of reasoning

Every paragraph should naturally produce the next one. Each idea should create the need for what follows, pulling the reader forward through reasoning rather than isolated sections, headings, or dramatic statements.

### 3. Reduce paragraph fragmentation

Use longer, connected paragraphs. Avoid unnecessary line breaks, isolated one-line paragraphs, pull quotes, callouts, and quote-like formatting. Write an essay, not a LinkedIn post.

### 4. Reveal reasoning instead of conclusions

Do not present the conclusion before the reader has travelled through the thinking. Let it emerge through observation, examples, questions, comparison, and reasoning so the destination feels earned.

### 5. Expand the lens

Begin with something specific, then widen the lens: AI evaluation might lead to human judgement, organisational behaviour, society, and human nature. Finish broader than the starting point.

### 6. Remove generic language

Avoid language that could appear unchanged in thousands of online posts, including:

- journey
- hustle
- grind
- game changer
- one thing I've learned
- one thing I noticed
- one thing I've been thinking about
- last week I found myself thinking
- in today's world
- we live in a world
- I'm excited
- incredibly
- amazing
- quietly
- powerful
- unlock
- leverage

Ask: **Could anyone have written this?** If the answer is yes, rewrite it.

### 7. Make the reasoning recognisably Teff's

The voice should be identifiable through its reasoning, not through repeated vocabulary or stylistic tricks. If Teff's name were removed, the paper should still feel recognisable.

### 8. Avoid “mic drop” writing

Do not use isolated dramatic ending sentences or write for applause. Arrive at the final insight naturally so the ending feels inevitable.

### 9. Preserve humility

The writing explores, observes, questions, and reasons. It does not preach. Readers should feel invited into the thinking process.

## Editorial test

Before considering a paper complete, answer all seven questions:

1. Does it begin naturally without announcing the reflection?
2. Does every paragraph lead naturally into the next?
3. Does it reveal thinking rather than conclusions?
4. Does it avoid generic LinkedIn language?
5. Does the paper expand its perspective before ending?
6. Would this still be worth reading five years from now?
7. If Teff's name were removed, would the writing still feel recognisable?

If any answer is **No**, rewrite the draft and repeat the test.

## Precedence and preservation

This is the default model for all future papers. It overrides conflicting wording guidance while preserving The Teff Papers' mission, vision, and core principles.

It does not authorise retrospective edits to approved or published papers. Existing work should remain unchanged unless a separate brief explicitly requests a revision.
